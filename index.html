ABSTRACT

The objective of this project is to study the behavior of fluids in motion and to verify Bernoulli's theorem. Fluid dynamics is a sub-discipline of fluid mechanics that deals with fluid flow—the science of liquids and gases in motion. This paper analyzes the conservation of energy in flowing fluids and demonstrates how pressure varies with velocity.

1. INTRODUCTION

Daniel Bernoulli (1700–1782) formulated the famous principle that bears his name. It essentially states that for an incompressible, non-viscous fluid in streamline flow, the sum of pressure energy, kinetic energy, and potential energy per unit volume remains constant.

This principle explains everyday phenomena such as the lifting of an airplane wing, the working of a Bunsen burner, and the curved path of a spinning cricket ball (Magnus Effect).

2. MATHEMATICAL DERIVATION

Consider a fluid moving through a pipe of varying cross-sectional area. Let $P_1$ and $P_2$ be the pressures at two ends.

$$ P + \frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho gh = \text{Constant} $$

Where:

  • $P$ = Static Pressure
  • $\rho$ (rho) = Density of the fluid
  • $v$ = Velocity of fluid flow
  • $h$ = Height relative to a reference point

According to the Work-Energy Theorem, the work done on the fluid is equal to the change in its mechanical energy. Ignoring viscosity ($ \eta = 0 $), the equation holds true for ideal fluids.

3. APPLICATIONS

3.1 The Venturi Meter

A device used to measure the rate of flow of liquid through a pipe. It works on the principle that when velocity increases, pressure decreases.

3.2 Dynamic Lift (Airfoil)

The shape of an aeroplane wing (aerofoil) is designed such that air flows faster over the top surface than the bottom. According to Bernoulli’s principle, $P_{top} < P_{bottom}$, creating an upward lift force.

4. CONCLUSION

Bernoulli's principle is a fundamental concept in fluid mechanics. However, it applies strictly to ideal fluids. In real-life scenarios, factors like viscosity, turbulence, and compressibility must be accounted for using the Navier-Stokes equations.
